Inspiration

A quote that stuck with us as we discussed this project was from Thinking in System by Donalla Meadows:

Living successfully in a world of complex systems means expanding not only our time horizons and thought horizons; above all, it means expanding the horizons of caring. There are moral reasons for doing that, of course. And if moral arguments are not sufficient, then systems thinking provides the practical reasons to back up the moral ones. The real system is interconnected. No part of the human race is separate either from other human beings or from the global ecosystem. It will not be possible in this integrated world for your heart to succeed if your lungs fail, or for your company to succeed if your workers fail, or for the rich in Los Angeles to succeed if the poor in Los Angeles fail, or for Europe to succeed if Africa fails, or for the global economy to succeed if the global environment fails. As with everything else about systems, most people already know about the interconnections that make moral and practical rules turn out to be the same rules. They just have to bring themselves to believe what they already know.

Team Essay

Our team ran with Meadows' concept of the horizon of caring and believed it maps directly onto the framework of a moral circle. We began our project by looking inward, evaluating our own boundaries of empathy. Despite hailing from vastly different cultures, our shared experiences as international students have stretched our personal moral circles outward. We found a deep, common alignment in how much weight we place on global humanity and the broader ecosystem.

However, when we projected this framework outward to analyze the default priorities of our home countries, we discovered a profound empathy gap. By mapping our personal values against national attitudes (as we perceived them) on an interactive radial chart, we visualized our most urgent challenge, which is the systemic hypocrisy that occurs when a nation's moral circle is stuck (rightly or not) to immediate local comfort or economic survival.

When moral concern is restricted to what is immediately visible, environmental destruction is easily justified as long as it happens out of sight. In Canada, citizens fiercely protect local forests while the nation quietly extracts and exports 4.5 million barrels of oil daily. Canadians champion climate awareness globally but resist domestic carbon taxes to fuel a car-centric culture where 97 percent of vehicles run on combustion engines. India presents an equally striking parallel. The country maintains strict local bans on cow slaughter for moral and religious reasons, yet operates as one of the world's largest exporters of water buffalo meat and leather. In Peru, the moral circle often contracts tightly around immediate family income. This localized focus is used to socially justify illegal gold mining in the Madre de Dios region, where miners use toxic mercury that permanently poisons rivers, treating the broader ecosystem as a distant concern. Portugal loudly rejected domestic oil drilling to protect its pristine coasts, but its citizens protest aggressively when fuel prices rise, relying heavily on importing dirty resources from poorer nations. Finally, in Taiwan, the existential drive to maintain global economic dominance means the government allows semiconductor giants like TSMC to consume 259,000 cubic meters of water daily, actively sacrificing local agriculture during severe droughts.

To rewrite this story, we believe the greatest lever for change is not a distant policy shift, but a sustained, deliberate disruption of the willful ignorance that thrives just beyond our horizon of caring. Ultimately, it starts with us. The empathy gap persists because it is comfortable to hide our heads in the sand when the true ecological costs of our lifestyles fall out of sight. Our lever is to keep these uncomfortable conversations alive and bring light to the systemic realities we mapped out today.

To drive this, our team relies on a distinct set of superpowers. By blending scientific methodology, cross cultural communication, and deep experience across a multitude of industries, we can ensure our presence in corporate environments means that they can’t look away. Whatever our future may hold as leaders, our goal is to translate abstract global threats into immediate operational priorities.

As we grow in our careers, our commitment is to remain acutely aware of this intentional lack of awareness in the corporate world. As Meadows points out, most people already know about the interconnections that make moral and practical rules turn out to be the same rules. They just have to bring themselves to believe what they already know. By continually pushing our own moral circles outward, we can challenge the organizations we lead to ensure our economic models finally reflect our shared global destiny.
